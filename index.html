Kohei Horikoshi was born in the Aichi Prefecture of Japan. At a young age, he developed a fascination with the fine arts, and drew frequently in his spare time. After high school, he attended Nagoya University of Arts, where he practiced drawing manga outside of the classroom, knowing that his ultimate goal was to become a successful mangaka.

His first job in manga was as an assistant to Yasuki Tanaka, writer of the popular manga “Summer Time Rendering.” In 2007, he earned a one-off spot for his manga to be featured in Shonen Jump, a widely-read manga anthology and magazine. Eventually, he was able to publish his first long form manga in 2010, called “Oumagadoki Zoo.”

He has written three different manga series, with the most well-known of the three being a series called “My Hero Academia.” This manga has is among the most popular manga of the 2010s, and has spawned numerous spin-offs in the form of tv shows, movies, and video games. It is based in a world where almost everyone has a special power, known as a “Quirk.”

Horikoshi has stated that his work is influenced by the manga he read as a child, as well as Marvel comics.

Oumagadoki Zoo (2010 - 2011)
Hana Anoi has just started her new job at her neighborhood Zoo. Pretty soon, she realizes that there is something a bit different about this particular zoo. The zoo director, Shina, was cursed by the ghost of a rabbit he killed, and must gather and care for every animal in the world in order to reverse the curse. Upon realizing this, Hana begins to help Shina in his quest, and her comprehensive knowledge of animals proves to be invaluable.
Barrage (2012)
Barrage is set on the planet of Industria, where war wages between aliens and humans. The story starts with a young boy named Astro, who works hard to provide food for his younger siblings. One day, Astro meets a boy who looks and talks just like him -- this boy turns out to be the Prince of Industria, who has run away from home. When he realizes their striking similarity, the prince asks Astro if he wants to act as his substitute, and Astro accepts!
My Hero Academia (2014 - Present)
This series is based in a world where most people have a "quirk", or special power. Some people abuse their power and become villains, while others, known as heroes, use their quirks to try to counter those villains. The best of the best are able to become professional heroes and gain popular recognition for their good deeds. Izuku is a teenage boy who aspires to be just like the amazing professional heroes he admired throuhgout his childhood. There is just one problem -- he was born without a quirk. One day, he meets the hero “All Might,” who, despite the strength of his quirk, is becoming weak and feeble due to an injury, and needs to transfer his power to a stronger vessel. Izuku ends up receiving All Might’s quirk, but the question is, will he be able to harness its massive power?

My Hero Academia and the Word-World

In The Importance of the Act of Reading, Freire (1983) explains that “Reading does not consist merely of decoding the written word or language; rather, it is preceded by and intertwined with knowledge of the world” (p. 1). It is interesting to consider how this applies to fantasy literature such as My Hero Academia, where a lot of the super-powered characters and events in the manga do not coincide with the real-life experience of its readers. One way that Horikoshi works to make My Hero Academia more relatable to young people’s lives is by placing some of the events of the book in a classroom context. For example, in order to achieve his dream of becoming a professional superhero, the main character must first graduate from a hero academy (as the title implies). This parallels the experiences of Horikoshi’s young readers, for whom success in school is often also a prerequisite for achieving their dreams. This is important for student’s engagement levels with My Hero Academia, because “one of the most common ways students interact with texts is by making personal connections with them” (Carillo, 2017, p. 1). Looking at other fantasy novels written for young people, one is able to see a similar technique used to great effect. For example, in the Harry Potter series, Rowling situated a world of spells and magical creatures inside the familiar academic context of a school called Hogwarts.

Comics: all plot, no purpose?

Manga is a very fast-paced genre of literature. Its graphic nature allows authors to show their readers action much faster than if they had to describe that action verbally. This is what makes good manga so engaging, but it comes with its dangers as well. Thiong’o (1993), while reflecting on a certain category of book he read when he was young, cautioned that “it was the strong action that made one forget, or swallow, all the racist epithets of the narratives.” I do not believe that Horishoki’s work is comparable to the colonial narratives found in the books that Thiong’o was referencing, but it is still important to consider what harmful beliefs the engaging plots may be hiding. Horikoshi grew up in Japan, which is a largely monoethnic country, and the ethnic makeup of his characters reflects this lack of diversity. What are the implications of bringing a book formed in a very racially homogenous environment into an environment with more diversity, such as an American public school? Questions like these would be important to discuss when providing Horikoshi’s work to students.

Critics of manga might claim that its graphic nature and fast-pace leads to a mindless reading experience, in which students are entertained, but not learning all that much. It is true that manga and comic books are not the most challenging reads, but maybe that isn’t necessarily a bad thing. In fact, for students who struggle to understand what they're reading, combining words and images is a great way to increase comprehension (Carter, 2008). Furthermore, including popular culture texts like comics or manga in classroom instruction can help to motivate reluctant readers (Hong Xu, 2008).

My Hero Academia and the "Chosen One" trope
In many fantasy novels written for youth, the main character is some sort of “Chosen One,” destined from birth to save the world in one way or another. One example of this is the popular Percy Jackson series, in which there is a famous prophecy predicting how and when Percy will save the day. In My Hero Academia, Horikoshi rejects this familiar “Chosen One” trope, and writes his main character as one of the few people who do not have a special “quirk” or power. Instead, it is Izuku’s dedication and persistence, along with some luck along the way, that allow him to be successful. This is a valuable departure from a common trope, because it emphasizes how your actions and circumstances may impact your path in life, rather than forwarding the idea of destiny or pre-determination.
Social Media and Public Image in My Hero Academia
In My Hero Academia, Horikoshi invites his readers to think about aspects of social media and public image in modern society. The best example of this is the hero “All Might,” who ends up transferring his power to the main character, Izuku. “All Might” gained popularity with the public due to his enormous power. However, several years before the events of My Hero Academia, he suffered an injury to his stomach that made him unable to eat enough food. Because of this, he only has the energy to perform hero work a few hours a day, and his body has become scrawny and weak. In order to maintain his image of invincibility, he hides his true form during his hero work, projecting the appearance of strength for as long as he can manage. In his first encounter with Izuku, All Might claims he hides his weakness because “a symbol of peace who saves people with a smile must never be daunted by evil” (Vol. 1, p. 40). In other words, he has to keep his public image in line with what his fans believe he represents, instead of showing them his honest self. This is an interesting dynamic to consider in the social-media era, where we decide every day what parts of ourselves we want to expose to the internet, and what parts should remain private. It also relates to the issue of people comparing their real selves with the idealized versions of celebrities they see online, in the same way that All Might’s fans might assume he doesn’t have the same problems as them, when in reality he is not as fearless as his public image might imply.
